Back to case studies Designing a design system: ComUnity Developer Toolkit

As a senior UX consultant at Pondo in 2023, I was brought in to help expand and embed consistent UX practice within the ComUnity Developer Toolkit team. Once onboard, I uncovered significant design and technical debt that was slowing development and creating friction across the workflow.

To address this, I championed and led the creation of a scalable design system—turning fragmented legacy designs into a shared framework that enabled faster delivery, better collaboration, and long-term UX consistency.

Objective

Embedding user-centred design into the product—and the process

ComUnity Developer Toolkit is a low-code platform for rapidly building custom digital tools. The product’s complexity—with its multi-step developer workflows—had grown over time, while UX input remained minimal. As the platform scaled, this led to increasing usability issues, inconsistencies, and delivery friction. In response, ComUnity brought in Pondo for long-term UX support.

I joined the existing Pondo team to expand their UX capacity—embedding more deeply within the product team to support scalable, sustainable design. Once onboard, it became clear that design was not yet integrated into the team workflow.

I inherited a fragmented Figma file created through years of isolated design efforts - it contained 365 screens, 100+ undocumented components and an outdated style guide. Without naming conventions or reusable structures, patterns were inconsistently applied and hard to maintain. This made handoff inefficient, introduced recurring friction, and blocked efforts to scale design efficiently.

My goal was to create a design system that bridged design and development, reduced technical debt, and laid the foundation for consistent, user-centred design. This case study focuses on that effort—part of a broader initiative by Pondo to bring clarity and structure to UX delivery.

The Figma file I inherited contained unstructured, inconsistent legacy screens and patterns - this needed to be resolved for to improve user experience and for design and delivery to scale efficiently.

My role in the team

Building design foundations in a developer-led team

As a Senior UX Consultant at Pondo, I worked alongside our UX Lead to expand design capacity on the ComUnity team. While the UX Lead focused on strategy, I was embedded day-to-day in a developer-led product team—collaborating with a product manager (also acting as CTO and product marketing lead), a delivery manager, and an engineering team with one front-end developer.

My role was to integrate design into the team’s delivery workflow and establish a scalable foundation for consistent, user-centred design. This included:

  • Leading the creation and maintenance of a scalable design system in Figma
  • Advocating for the design system’s value to both technical and non-technical stakeholders
  • Promoting user-centred interaction patterns and inclusive design across a complex, developer-focused product
  • Collaborating closely with developers to align design and implementation
  • Conducting informal validation of the design system with peer developers to ensure usability and relevance

Excerpt from the organisation diagram highlighting the key decision-makers in the developer-led product team: the Product Owner/CTO and the Lead Engineer.

Constraints and challenges

Balancing foundational work with fast-paced delivery

Building a design system in a high-velocity, developer-led environment came with several constraints. UX was welcomed in principle, but not yet embedded in process or resourced for long-term impact. While balancing hands-on execution with systems thinking, I navigated the following conditions:

Feature delivery was a priority
  • The team was initially cautious about whether a design system would support or slow delivery.
  • We worked from existing personas and earlier in-house research, as no new user research was commissioned during this phase.
  • Design was valued, but dedicating time to process required advocacy—and was often deprioritised in favour of delivery speed.
Managing legacy design debt
  • I inherited a large, unstructured Figma file—365 screens and 108 components with no documentation or naming conventions.
  • The current implementation did not consider responsivity or accessibility.
  • Figma was the sole design tool, requiring all systemisation, documentation, and collaboration to happen within that environment.
Limited capacity, fast-paced setting
  • The design system was developed in parallel with ongoing feature work, without a dedicated sprint.
  • Time and budget were limited, and the front-end team was lean.
  • As an enterprise-grade, highly configurable platform, the product required a flexible, scalable approach to design patterns

The approach

Laying the groundwork for scalable design

The design system needed to support fast-paced delivery without slowing the team down. To make this possible, we focused on gaining buy-in, working collaboratively, and laying just enough structure to guide consistent, user-centred design—without requiring a full overhaul of the UI.

We mapped desired outcomes and existing barriers, then pinpointed interventions to bridge the gap.

1

Framing the design system as a shared tool for improving efficiency:

I positioned the design system as a way to reduce reliance on mockups, clarify implementation, and support faster onboarding—framing it as a scalable, low-friction solution to improve quality without slowing delivery.

2

Focusing on structured, incremental improvements:

I began by auditing the existing UI to identify common, high-impact patterns and inconsistencies. The focus was on foundational elements that could deliver immediate value without slowing the team down.

3

Recognising that adoption was key, we brought developers into the process early:

I held walkthroughs with developers to align on structure and usage, asked how components were built and reused in code, and used their feedback to validate the system’s logic and naming conventions.

4

Making Figma the Single Source of Truth:

With Figma as our sole tool, we built a centralised, flexible system that supported both design and development. We applied atomic principles, used interactive states to show behaviour, and structured files for clarity and reuse. Embedded documentation guided usage, making Figma a shared, evolving source of truth across the team.

Execution

1. Audit: Understanding the foundation before rebuilding

After reviewing the existing user personas as part of the audit process, I documented insights and created a set of design principles to help us move forward.

  • Catalogued inconsistencies:

    Audited 365 screens and 108 components - Inconsistencies in spacing, typography, colour use, and interaction patterns were catalogued.

  • Learned that UI styling had been driven by brand colours:

    This was discovered through a styling alignment workshop with the product owner.

  • Key UI elements didn't meet WCAG AA contrast standards

    I conducted an accessibility review. Buttons and navigation were not designed to meet readability standards. There were also no component states or focus states, with few broader inclusive design measures in place.

  • I revisited the existing personas and early research:

    I put together a set of design principles to help us move forward. This was based on insights from the existing research.

  • I spoke with the engineers to understand their pain points:

    The biggest one? Not knowing which components or patterns to use—especially when it came to displaying information or giving system feedback.

2. Communicating issues and advocating for user-centred processes

Excerpts from the presentation I gave at the accessibility workshop, where we made a proposal to improve readability.

  • We shared the audit findings with stakeholders:

    We proposed a Phase 1 design system focused on documenting core components and patterns - we got buy-in to move forward.

  • We got buy-in to proceed with incremental accessibility improvements:

    In an accessibility workshop, I highlighted WCAG standards and made the case for designing with edge-case users in mind. Colour contrast was a key issue—brand colours used in navigation and buttons didn’t meet standards. We got support to adjust key colours immediately and include broader accessibility updates in the future roadmap

3. Structuring scalable components

  • I created a structure around core foundations:

    A system for colour, typography and spacing were set up using Figma variables, incorporating accessibility improvements. An icon library was set up.

  • UI elements were created from the ground up:

    Atoms (buttons, inputs), molecules (form fields, nav items), and organisms (headers, sidebars)

Above: I used Figma variables to set up a foundation for the design system, allowing control and flexibility.

Atomic design was used in the planning and execution of the design system

  • I structured reusable, scalable components around key interface elements

    I focused on navigation, modals, and core UI patterns—using clear naming conventions and Figma variants to show interactive states.

I built a library of reusable and interactive components.

4. Figma as a living source of truth.

  • The Figma design system became the central hub for both design and engineering:

    I embedded documentation and pattern guidelines directly into the file, and gave engineers access via Dev Mode so they could explore and implement without relying on handoff decks.

  • The team treated the file as a living document:

    We discussed and refine patterns together through regular walkthroughs and weekly feedback sessions.

Above: Documentation for components and interactions were embedded into the Figma file.

Results

What changed? Small system, big shifts

1

Faster, cleaner delivery:

Developers adopted the system early—especially the frontend developer and lead platform engineer—which meant they could implement faster and more independently. It reduced back-and-forth, cut ambiguity, and freed us up to focus on higher-value design work.

2

Consistent UX, without the chaos:

By centralising patterns and decisions, the product started to feel more cohesive—even as new features shipped at speed.

3

Design became part of the team—not an add-on:

The system gave us a shared point of reference, which helped integrate UX into delivery and build real trust across roles.

4

A springboard for improvement:

With the system in place, we had a practical foundation for incremental UX improvements across the board—without needing a full UI overhaul.

5

Long-term partnership:

Pondo has remained embedded in the team since 2022, working to deepen UX integration as the product evolves.

However, there is still a way to go on accessibility, responsiveness and user research...

While we secured buy-in for foundational improvements, accessibility improvements and updated user research remain lower on the team’s list—often sidelined by the pressure to ship new features. I know these areas are essential to delivering a truly usable and useful product.

Lessons

What I learned and what I could have done better

  • Meet your stakeholders where they are at:

    One key learning was the need to tailor communication to match stakeholder priorities. The team was highly output-driven—after a few early workshops that didn’t quite land, I realised that leading with UX terminology risked creating disconnect. I shifted my approach, framing design recommendations in terms of practical outcomes like faster delivery and reduced developer friction—aligning with what mattered most to them.

  • One area I’d approach differently is pushing for updated user research earlier in the process:

    While it was technically out of scope, I see now that even a few quick validation interviews could’ve sharpened our decisions and strengthened our position when advocating for design improvements.

  • I came away from the project wanting to grow my ability to articulate value:

    Especially around accessibility and research. The experience left me asking: how can I better connect these priorities to the outcomes stakeholders care about? It made me want to deepen my skills not just in delivery, but in influencing and advocacy.

  • A design system is only as effective as the discipline behind how it’s built and used:

    This project taught me a lot about Figma—especially as new features like Dev Mode, variables, and advanced component capabilities were rolling out. But the real learning was about the importance of maintenance. In trying to move fast, I didn’t fully take advantage of Figma’s component configurability upfront. Balancing system work with day-to-day design delivery made it tempting to skip some setup steps. Looking back, I wish I’d invested a little more time early on—because it would’ve saved effort later, and made the system even easier to scale.

Designing systems, not just screens.
