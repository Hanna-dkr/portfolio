Back to case studies Aligning policy with practice: Co-creating inclusive marketplaces

In 2013, The City of Cape Town identified an unsafe transport interchange that was home to hundreds of informal traders as a key site for urban regeneration. As part of the team responsible for designing the regeneration strategy, my role was to research the lived experiences of these traders and surface the critical insights that would be used to shape policy and a strategy grounded in inclusion, safety and economic resilience.

Objective

Congestion, crime, and policy misalignment—can human-centred design drive systemic change?

This project, based in Nyanga’s main transport hub—one of the most crime-affected areas in Cape Town—explored that question directly. The brief was not simply for spatial regeneration, but a complex, multi-scalar intervention.

SUN Development was brought in for our holistic approach, combining spatial design with policy-level thinking, and a commitment to co-design and community participation.

The municipality sought safer, more structured public environments—but the area’s realities demanded more than top-down planning.

Informal trade, congestion, and mistrust in governance pointed to deeper systemic misalignments. Our objective was to understand and respond to those dynamics through research, systems analysis, and participatory design—ensuring that any intervention worked for the people who used the space daily, not just on paper.

My role in the team

Researching the trading network and translating findings into inclusive design principles

As an architect on the team, I played a key role in executing research, synthesizing findings, and co-developing strategy—critical to driving a multidisciplinary urban regeneration approach. This involved:

  • Leading interviews with traders, conducting qualitative and quantitative field research, and facilitating participatory sessions
  • Collaborating with community facilitators and leading interns during on-site research
  • Mapping stakeholder needs and pain points across diverse user groups
  • Producing artefacts—maps, reports, infographics—to communicate insights
  • Collaborating with the urban design lead to create design principles that would inform the trading plan and regeneration strategy

Project stakeholder map: During the research and design process I focused on the experience of traders, also engaging many of the stakeholders that directly or indirectly impacted them.

The approach

A participatory, systems-based approach to research and design.

Photo of co-design session with traders, including myself—evidence of a participatory, inclusive process. Photo credit: SUN Development

1. Understand the status quo and the lived experience of traders:

I worked closely with community facilitators and team members to conduct qualitative and quantitative field research within a systems thinking framework. I lead the research component that focused on traders.

2. Develop inclusive principles to advocate for the needs of traders:

I developed trader-specific design principles based on research insights, in collaboration with the lead urban designer.

3. Design concept plans through a participatory process:

I collaborated with two urban designers and an urban planner to create concept plans. We facilitated co-design sessions with traders and city stakeholders, allowing us to validate and integrate the design principles.

Execution

The research process:

Immersive fieldwork and multi‑stakeholder engagement

1
Understanding trade in context
  • We began with a review of municipal data to understand the area's socio-economic landscape and spatial systems.
  • Observational fieldwork:We studied the daily rhythm of informal trade in relation to commuter flows, movement patterns, infrastructure, and friction points.
  • Stakeholder walkabouts:We engaged with traders, residents, municipal officials, and law enforcement to hear firsthand accounts of the area’s challenges.

We gathered stories from stakeholders on site walkabouts. Photo: SUN Development.

2
I led a focused investigation into how informal trade functioned on the ground
  • Mapping daily operations:I guided the fieldwork process, working with two interns to map 365 trader stalls and gather quantitative data around location, typology, operations, and spatial needs.
  • Interviewing traders to gather qualitative data: I created interview scripts. We conducted 20 in-depth interviews with a representative sample of traders from different groups. I led the analysis of findings, identifying key themes around business structure, pain points, and daily practices.
  • Quantitative data integration: I reviewed the trader census data gathered by a separate team within our oganisation and used it to enrich our understanding .

We interview traders (notes pictured above) and analysed their trading operations in detail. Photo: SUN Development.

3
Policy & precedent review
  • Municipal by-laws: We interviewed city officials and reviewed the applicable by-laws to understand friction points and areas of trader non-compliance.
  • Explored successful local examples of trader-government partnership: I represented the design team on a field trip to Warwick Junction in Durban - We interviewed traders and government officials to understand the inner workings of their partnership.

On site in Warwick Junction, learning about government–trader partnerships.

The research insights:

Structural fragility, lived risk and misaligned policy

1
Criminals targeted traders - women faced elevated risk
"There are lots of skollies (thieves)… We try to protect each other and hired a security guard together. We don’t have any storage—it’s a problem. Anything left in the stall at night gets stolen."
– Female meat trader, Nyanga
  • Traders were robbed in their stalls or on their way home from work—streets were poorly lit. They didn't have access to storage so would make the journey carrying their goods and earnings.
  • Traders were instrumental in helping us to identify crime hotspots in the area.

Traders were vulnerable to crime - especially women.

2
Policy didn't align with reality and compliance was nearly impossible

Above: I created this diagram to illustrate our research findings on the root causes of trader non-compliance with informal municipal trading regulations.

  • Permit options were not flexible enough: Regulation allowed one permit per household. On the ground, ownership was often shared between extended family members or business partners, reflecting the cultural norm of sharing resources.
  • Compliance was impossible for food traders: They didn't have access to the infrastructure they needed to get a Certificate of Acceptibility (electricity, water, drainage, smoke exctraction, waste disposal...). They made up 30% of the traders in the are.
  • Regulations allowed each permit holder access to a single rental space, but this didn’t reflect the realities of how traders actually operated: Many needed additional space for storage, food preparation, and managing deliveries. Without it, trade spilled over into public areas out of necessity.
3
Relocating traders would risk their livelihoods
  • Traders were concerned about being relocated - this could sever them from their client base and cooperative working relationships.
  • Traders were self-organised and cooperative - those selling similar products had formed groups and had systems in place for security and sharing resources.
  • 52% of the 365 audited traders had worked from the same kiosk for over 11 years —some had been there for more than 35 years. Traders had carefully chosen their locations based on foot traffic and local opportunities.

Location mattered—traders had long-standing customer bases and working relationships tied to the spots they’d occupied for years.

4
If trade breaks down, the supply chain and community both suffer
  • The community needed traders because they provided a vital service - Only one formal mall was available in the broad area, to support 58 000 people (Census 2011). Traders made goods accessible in affordable quantities.
  • Trade supported livelihoods well beyond the market itself - relied on informal supply chains, sustaining the local economy and provided vital jobs in an area with high unemployment.

Design principles informed by research:

Putting traders at the heart of the renewal proposal

We created design principles to help stakeholders operationalise the research findings. Our team also used them to guide our participatory design process.

I collaborated with the lead urban designer to develop six principles that advocated for the needs of traders at an infrastructural, policy and implementation level.

Implement the renewal in phases

Plan and negotiate the phasing of construction with traders, so that trade is continuous.

Ensure that alternative locations are available during construction to minimise disruption.

Support compliance at existing trade locations

Only relocate traders when essential.

Co-design infrastructural improvements to ensure that they are suitable and align with operations.

Create a destination market with infrastructure

Create a high-visibility marketplace at the interchange accommodating complex needs.

Trader feedback on layouts is critical.

Well-lit, pedestrian routes designed for safety

Deliver lighting, designated pedestrian zones, and passive surveillance.

Support trade with small public spaces.

Adjust policy to support reality on the ground

Include flexible permitting options for shared ownership.

Propose rental-bay sizes matching actual needs.

Ongoing participation and partnership

Embed long-term community engagement in the management model.

Reframe the city’s role as partner, not just regulator.

Navigating challenges

Design under pressure: Trust, language, and institutional silos

Building trust with traders

Challenge: Our link to the municipality initially created skepticism. Language barriers and the presence of police escorts added tension, with the risk of skewing research.

Remedy: Close collaboration with community facilitators was key to build trust and ensure informed participation.

Inclusive communication

Challenge: Using conventional architectural methods to communicate ideas during participatory design sessions excluded participants who were not trained in the field.

Remedy: We evolved our methods - we used walkabouts, storytelling and tactile methods to ensure participants were able to contribute and co-create.

Siloed governance

Challenge: Decision making on integrated solutions was slowed down by municipal silos with conflicting agendas. Existing policy was also a barrier at times.

Remedy: We brought key stakeholders together in varying combinations to discuss challenges and possible solutions. Whenever possible we encouraged meeting on site, where the project was tangible.

Deliverables

A strategy grounded in lived experience

Deliverables shaped by stakeholder engagement:

After an extensive participatory process with stakeholders we delivered a trading plan and concept plans for a phased upgrade of the area. The concept plan was supported by recommendations for land release, business modelling and area based management.

We developed a phased trading plan that addressed key policy considerations. With the support of City stakeholders, the plan was submitted for planning approval.

We ran a series of participatory design engagements. I played a key role in facilitating co-design sessions with traders. The central photo was taken during one such session. Photo credit: SUN Development.

Deliverable #1 - The trading plan:

We developed a phased trading plan that addressed key policy considerations. With the support of City stakeholders, the plan was submitted for planning approval.

I collaborated with the economic development specialist leading the trading plan, contributing to the development of rental bay options and designing a phased implementation layout that ensured all traders were accommodated.

The trading plan proposal and layouts provided a roadmap towards compliance, working with current conditions as a starting point.

Deliverable #2 - The concept plan:

Complimenting the trading plan, we delivered a phased concept plan. This plan guided the development of infrastructure needs, access routes, safe walkways, zoning, parking and other spatial, social and economic informants.

These images were produced by the team, but included my work and contributions. They were intended to help the City scope the project and were submitted with techical proposals for land-release and business modelling.

  • Phase 1 proposed the regularisation of trade, emphasizing the importance of improving the existing trade locations, providing basic services to traders and meeting some of their critical needs, like lighting and public restrooms.
  • Phase 2 focused on creating safe public walkways and public spaces to connect the area and support trade. This also involved further infrastructure upgrades for traders.
  • Phase 3 focused on the implementation of a destination trading market at the PTI , that would provide infrastructure to allow food traders to be fully compliant.

Results

What happened next?

NOTHING...

Nothing substantial happened for seven years.

The project was paused in 2015 after the Trading Plan was submitted for approval, due to issues with budget allocation. In 2016, the City adopted a new Transit Oriented Development Strategy. They could revisit the project and included it in an application for grant funding, which was approved in 2019.

In 2021, the City returned to SUN Development and contracted the organisation to re-audit the traders and update the concept plans, with the intention of implementing in 2022.

I followed up on the project outcome in April 2025 - a tender process had been initiated, indicating progress toward implementation. SUN Development was actively involved, working to keep traders and leaders engaged.

But something, actually...

Despite the slow pace, the project delivered meaningful results.

  • The stakeholder engagement process itself was a win. It shifted the conversation from “compliance” to “support.” This was shown by the City's endorsement of the Trading Plan, indicating a policy-level shift—recognising complexity.
  • The City’s return to SUN Development after years of inactivity showed that the original work had value. That decision—to pick up the project and re-engage traders—points to longer-term institutional learning.

In short: not a transformation, but a recalibration. Small shifts that move us closer to re-making South African cities—safer, more inclusive, and more honest about the inequalities they were built on.

Lessons

What I learned from this project

1

Proximity reveals complexity

Working closely with traders dismantled convenient binaries—formal/informal, legal/illegal, public/private. They weren’t operating outside the system; they were a key reason it was able to function. This project pushed me to look beyond surface symptoms and see how deeply rooted systems shape what we too easily frame as ‘problems.’

2

Listening shifts power - and changes what gets built

This project taught me to recognise the depth of lived expertise—knowledge shaped by experience, not credentials. Listening to traders wasn’t just a method for collecting insights; it was a way to make space for them to define their own realities. In a system that often sidelines or speaks over them, being heard carried real weight. As someone involved in shaping outcomes, I came to understand that listening isn’t neutral—it redistributes influence. When the people most affected help shape the story, the outcomes are more grounded—and they work better.

3

Participation can come at a cost - especially for the vulnerable

After working closely with traders over two years, I’ve kept coming back to the trust they placed in us. They shared insight, lived experience and time—often during their working hours. They were willing participants, knowing that the outcome of the project could meaningfully affect their livelihoods. Although we were transparent about the uncertainties around implementation, I sometimes wonder if the process asked too much of them, especially given the long delays that followed. This experience reminded me that incentivisation in design isn’t just about tokens or stipends. It’s about understanding what’s at stake for the people involved, and taking responsibility for the weight of their trust—especially when outcomes are slow, partial, or unclear.

4

Facilitating inclusive engagement

Facilitating participatory workshops taught me how to create space for different voices—especially those often excluded from formal processes. It required active listening, flexibility, and the ability to guide discussion without dominating it. Working closely with the community facilitator and translator was essential—not just for communication, but for building trust and cultural understanding. Through this, I learned how to surface tensions respectfully, balance competing needs, and keep conversations focused and meaningful, even when the topics were complex or emotionally charged.

Designing systems, not just screens.
